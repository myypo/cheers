# Brand register

Use this when design is the product: landing pages, marketing surfaces, campaign pages, portfolios, public storytelling, long-form content, and brand moments where the visitor's impression is the deliverable.

## Brand slop test

If someone could look at the page and say "AI made that" without hesitation, it failed. Brand surfaces need a point of view, a specific audience, and a willingness to risk strangeness when the brief calls for it.

Before committing, name the aesthetic lane. A technical product launch, a luxury hotel, a music campaign, and a research lab can all be brand surfaces, but they should not share the same beige editorial template.

Run the inverse test: describe the concept in one sentence the way a competitor would. If the sentence fits the average page in the category, restart.

## Typography

### Font selection

1. Write three concrete brand-voice words. Use physical qualities such as warm, mechanical, ceremonial, clinical, restless, tactile. Avoid empty words like modern or clean.
2. Name the fonts you would reach for by reflex, then challenge them. Training-data defaults create monoculture.
3. Choose type as if it were a physical object: museum caption, terminal manual, fabric label, concert poster, receipt, field guide, laboratory tag.
4. Cross-check the category reflex. Technical does not automatically mean mono. Elegant does not automatically mean serif.

### Pairing and scale

- Use two families only when voice needs it. A single well-chosen family with strong size and weight contrast can be more distinctive.
- Use fluid display sizing and stronger scale contrast when the surface earns it.
- Light text on dark backgrounds needs more line-height and often stronger weight.
- Avoid repeated tiny uppercase labels unless they are a deliberate brand grammar.

## Color

Brand surfaces can use **Committed**, **Full palette**, and **Drenched** strategies. Use them when they serve the story.

- Name a real reference before picking a palette.
- Palette is voice. A calm brand and a restless brand should not share palette mechanics.
- Do not hedge when the strategy is Committed or Drenched.
- If a cultural-symbol palette is obvious, reach past it. Let typography, imagery, and copy carry the cultural signal.

## Layout

- Centered stacks are a default, not a concept.
- Use asymmetry, strict grids, visible structure, oversized type, generous pacing, or single-purpose sections when they match the brand.
- Vary rhythm across sections. Brand pages can breathe, compress, pause, and accelerate.
- Cards are valid only when they are the right affordance or the chosen visual grammar.

## Imagery

Image-led briefs need imagery. Restaurants, hotels, travel, fashion, photography, products, communities, and places usually cannot be solved with colored rectangles and cards.

- Use real project assets when available.
- One decisive image beats five mediocre ones.
- Alt text is part of voice and context.
- Imagery can also be product screenshots, data visualization, generated SVG, canvas, WebGL, or illustration when that is the actual visual world.

## Motion

- Brand can use larger reveals, scroll moments, typographic choreography, and atmospheric effects.
- Motion still needs purpose, performance, and reduced-motion alternatives.
- One memorable motion idea beats scattered micro-interactions.

## Brand bans

- Monospace as lazy shorthand for technical.
- Large rounded icons above every heading as a template move.
- Timid palettes and average layouts when the brief needs identity.
- Zero imagery when the subject implies imagery.
- Editorial magazine aesthetics by default.
- Repeating the same kicker, card, and metric grammar across every section.

## Brand permissions

- Typographic risk.
- Unexpected color strategies.
- Single-purpose folds and deliberate pacing.
- Art direction per section when the narrative demands it.
- A signature visual motif that would be too loud for product UI.
